Week 4


Corel Draw and Using the Laser!


For week four, I learned how to use Corel draw and different laser cutting/engraving technique. After I got my deisgns down, I made sure to hairline what I wanted to vector and left the stuff I wanted to raster alone. To vector means to cut on the laser while raster is to engrave.

What I Made

First, I measured out the clock piece I was using with a Vernier scale. A Vernier scale helped me find the exact width of the clock piece. This helped me fit it properly when cutting my design. First, I took a picture of a bunch of bare trees from a winter day. Then, I got a clip-art design of a snowflake. I made a circle with a diameter of 10 and PowerCliped the trees inside of the circle frame. I took the frame of the circle and selected it. I hairlined it afterwards and started hairlining my snowflakes. I hairlined them so they would be recognized by the laser that they needed to be cut out. Once my design was complete, I saved it as a .cdr to a flashdrive and brought it over to the Epilog Laser. I plugged it into the computer that ran with the laser and opened my file through Corel Draw again. I hit print and got this screen.


After I got this screen, I hit preferences.

Preferences allowed me to change the laser's settings to fit my needs. I needed to cut and engrave a 12x24 inch piece of 1/8 thick wood. I needed to change the settings of the laser so that I could raster and vector it properly. For rastering (engraving), I needed 50 for speed and 100 for power. For vectoring (cutting), I needed to have 8 for speed, 100 for power, and 20 for frequency. Then I changed the length of my board in the piece size section. This is very important, especially since it starts with the height and width of a piece of paper, 11x8.5. I made sure to change the settings to have 24 inches for the width and 12 inches for the height. Then I pressed okay and applied my settings. I made sure that the printer I was sending it to was the Epilog and hit print.
